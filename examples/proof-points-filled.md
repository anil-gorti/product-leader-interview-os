## Series B SaaS Company — Head of Product — 3 Years

### Context
B2B workflow automation platform serving mid-market financial services.
120 employees when I joined, 280 when I left. Hired to lead the
transition from SMB to enterprise product, including rebuilding the team.

### The Primary Bet
Invested 40% of engineering capacity in a data intelligence layer
on top of the existing workflow product, rather than closing
feature gaps with the incumbent competitor. This was the decision
the board questioned most and the one that produced the most
durable return.

### The Metrics
- Enterprise NRR: 94% → 118% over 18 months
- Time-to-first-value (enterprise segment): 45 days → 12 days
- CS-escalated support tickets: reduced 38% without adding headcount
- Intelligence layer adoption (90-day): 67% of enterprise accounts

### The Moat / Compound Effect
The intelligence layer improves with data volume. Each account that
uses it for 12 months produces better predictions than it did at
month 1. Competitors cannot replicate this without the equivalent
transaction history. The moat widens automatically.

## Series A Startup — First PM — 2 Years

### Context
Consumer fintech in India. 8 engineers, no product function when I joined.
Built the PM function from zero. Raised Series B 14 months after I joined.

### The Primary Bet
Prioritised the mobile-native flow (UPI + WhatsApp notifications) over
the web product, against strong internal disagreement. The web product
was more complete. The mobile flow was where the target user actually lived.

### The Metrics
- D7 retention: 22% → 41% after mobile-native redesign
- Activation rate (install to first transaction): 31% → 58%
- Series B raise: ₹65 crore [this was 9 months after mobile pivot]

### The Moat / Compound Effect
The mobile-first architecture attracted a user segment that the
web product was structurally unable to serve. Competitor could not
replicate without rebuilding from a different foundation.

---

## The Pattern Across Both
In both cases: the durable return came from an investment in a
data or architecture layer that competitors could not replicate
because it required accumulation over time, not just engineering effort.
The short-term pressure was always to build visible features that closed
the competitive gap. The long-term outcome came from building the
layer that made the competitive gap irrelevant.
