# C-Suite Objections — India Specific

The objections below are the ones that appear most frequently  
in senior product leader interviews at Indian companies.  
Each has the underlying concern, the trap version, and the frame that resolves it.

---

## "You have not operated at this scale before"

**Underlying concern**: The company is at a stage or size that  
exceeds the candidate's direct experience. The CEO wants to know  
whether the candidate will be overwhelmed or whether they have  
a clear model for how to operate at the new level.

**The trap**: Defending past scale or minimising the gap.  
Both responses confirm the concern rather than resolving it.

**The frame that works**: Name the gap directly, then argue  
that the relevant credential is not scale-matching but  
architecture instinct. The PM who has designed a compounding  
platform at 50 engineers has the instinct that scales.  
The PM who has managed a 200-person org without that instinct  
is a manager, not a product leader.

---

## "The team you would inherit is already strong"

**Underlying concern**: The CEO is signalling that they do not  
want the new product leader to disrupt a functioning team.  
But the subtext is often the opposite: the team is not strong  
enough for the next stage and the CEO needs a product leader  
who will diagnose that honestly rather than protect it.

**The trap**: Agreeing too quickly that the team is strong,  
which surrenders the diagnostic credibility that makes  
the role valuable.

**The frame that works**: "Strong teams at the current stage  
and strong teams at the next stage are sometimes different teams.  
My job in the first 30 days is to understand which parts of the  
current team have the ceiling for where you are going, and to  
be honest with you about what I find — because the worst outcome  
is discovering that gap at month 12 instead of month 2."

---

## "Why are you available? What went wrong?"

**Underlying concern**: At Director level and above, availability  
signals something in Indian corporate culture. The assumption  
is that strong product leaders at senior levels are not  
actively looking — they are being recruited.

**The trap**: Defensive explanation. Over-justification.  
Any version of "it's complicated" that does not resolve the concern.

**The frame that works**: Name the transition type precisely.  
If it is voluntary: "I completed the mandate I was hired for.  
The next challenge for this company is a different kind of problem  
than the one I am best at." If it is a founder-building stage:  
"I have been building [X] as a founder. I am at the point  
where the specific problem [target company] is solving  
is the most important place for my capability."

---

## "You spent [X years] outside India. Can you operate here?"

**Underlying concern**: The CEO has seen senior product leaders  
return from the US and apply US product culture without  
translation, producing team friction and attrition.

**The trap**: Over-claiming India familiarity you do not have.  
Or dismissing the concern as cultural stereotyping.

**The frame that works**: Demonstrate specific knowledge  
of the translation required. Name the gaps — authority deference,  
conviction gap, bodies trap — and describe specifically how  
you address them. The product leader who has a vocabulary  
for the cultural translation problem has already solved  
more than half of it.

---

## "What if the team resists your direction?"

**Underlying concern**: The CEO has seen product leader hires  
fail because the incoming leader tried to change everything  
at once, lost the team's trust, and spent six months in  
recovery mode.

**The trap**: Promising that you will not change things too fast  
(which sounds like you will not lead) or that you have strong  
leadership skills (which is generic and unconvincing).

**The frame that works**: "Resistance usually means one of  
three things: the team does not understand the direction,  
the team does not trust that the direction is right, or  
the team does not believe they have the capability to execute it.  
Each one requires a different response. The first 30 days  
is about diagnosing which one is actually happening  
before proposing anything that requires buy-in."

---

## "You are overqualified for this role"

**Underlying concern**: The CEO is worried about retention.  
They have hired someone senior before, watched them get bored  
at month eight, and leave at month twelve when something bigger  
appeared.

**The trap**: Arguing that you are not overqualified  
(which implies you do not see the concern) or promising  
you will stay (which is exactly what the last senior hire promised).

**The frame that works**: Name the specific mandate that  
makes this role the right level of challenge for you right now.  
The platform transition, the category definition moment,  
the team rebuild — whatever is genuinely true. If none of those  
are present, the concern might be correct and the role might  
not be the right one.

---

## "Why do you want to work at a startup when you have been at large companies?"

**Underlying concern**: The candidate might struggle with ambiguity,  
resource scarcity, and the absence of the support structures  
that large companies provide. The CEO does not want a candidate  
who is fleeing corporate bureaucracy and has romanticised startup life.

**The trap**: Criticising your previous company.  
Or describing startup life as exciting without demonstrating  
that you understand what it actually requires.

**The frame that works**: Describe the specific type of problem  
you do best — and demonstrate that it is precisely the type  
of problem this company is facing right now. The candidate  
who can say "this stage, this constraint, this architectural  
decision — this is the environment where my instinct is sharpest"  
has answered the concern without defending against it.
