# Skill: Team Diagnostics

## Trigger
"How do I assess the PM team I am inheriting"
"What does the 30-day diagnostic look like"
"Help me prepare the capability assessment section"
"What questions do I ask each PM in week one"

## The Diagnostic Questions for Week One
Ask each PM four questions directly:

1. "What is the metric you own and what did it do last quarter?"
   — Tests: commercial accountability, whether they think in outcomes
   
2. "Tell me about the last customer conversation you had.
   What did you learn that surprised you?"
   — Tests: customer proximity, discovery as practice vs project
   
3. "What is the one thing on your roadmap you would kill if
   you could, and why hasn't it been killed?"
   — Tests: intellectual honesty, editing discipline, political awareness
   
4. "What decision are you most uncertain about right now?"
   — Tests: self-awareness, comfort with uncertainty, problem framing

## The Engineering Team Diagnostic
Ask the engineering lead one question:
"What is the architectural decision in your system you are
least proud of — and what would it take to fix it?"

The answer tells you: technical debt location, whether engineering
has product input into architecture, and the engineering lead's
intellectual honesty about the current state.

## Red Flags at Senior Level
- PM who cannot name their commercial metric
- PM who last spoke to a customer more than two weeks ago
- PM whose problem statements describe features, not customer accountabilities
- Engineering team that has been making architecture decisions without
  product input for more than three months
- Any team member who says "we tried that before" as a complete objection

## Output Format
One assessment card per PM: strength / gap / category (1/2/3) / decision by day 60
Engineering diagnostic: the three architectural risks and their product implications
The bodies trap locations: where is headcount substituting for product design?
The quick wins: 3-5 things that could be changed in days 31-60 that the team
   would immediately feel as a signal that something has changed
